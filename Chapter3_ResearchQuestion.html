Chapter 3 — From Problem Area to Research Question#

Purpose#

This chapter focuses on one of the most critical and difficult steps in scientific research: transforming a broad area of interest into a precise, feasible, and researchable question. In AI and Data Science, many projects fail not because of poor implementation, but because the research problem is ill-defined, overly broad, or misaligned with scientific contribution.

By the end of this chapter, students will understand how to move from general curiosity or application ideas to well-scoped research questions and hypotheses that can be addressed rigorously within the constraints of a Master’s-level thesis.

Learning Objectives#

After completing this chapter, you will be able to:

  • Distinguish between problem areas, research problems, research questions, and hypotheses.

  • Identify meaningful and researchable problems in AI and Data Science.

  • Assess novelty, feasibility, and relevance of potential research questions.

  • Formulate clear research objectives and hypotheses aligned with the scientific method.

  • Scope a research question appropriately for an MS-level thesis.

  • Align research questions with available data, methods, and evaluation strategies.

1. From Interest Areas to Research Problems#

Students often begin with broad interests such as:

  • “Explainable AI”

  • “Bias in machine learning”

  • “Large language models”

  • “AI in healthcare”

While these areas are important, they are not research problems. A research problem must identify:

  • A gap, limitation, or unresolved issue

  • A context in which the problem manifests

  • Why existing approaches are insufficient

A useful heuristic is:

What do we still not understand, measure, explain, or validate in this area?

2. Characteristics of a Good Research Problem#

A strong AI/DS research problem is:

  • Clear: precisely stated and unambiguous

  • Focused: narrow enough to be studied rigorously

  • Grounded: supported by gaps in the literature

  • Feasible: solvable with available data, tools, and time

  • Relevant: scientifically and/or societally meaningful

Problems that are too broad, purely engineering-focused, or lacking empirical grounding often lead to weak scientific contributions.

3. Research Questions vs. Objectives vs. Hypotheses#

3.1 Research Questions#

A research question articulates what you want to understand or explain.

Examples:

  • How does distribution shift affect the fairness of classification models?

  • What trade-offs exist between explainability and performance in deep learning models?

Research questions should be answerable through empirical or experimental methods.

3.2 Research Objectives#

Objectives describe what the study aims to accomplish.

Examples:

  • To evaluate model robustness under synthetic distribution shifts

  • To compare explainability techniques across model architectures

Objectives are often multiple and operational.

3.3 Hypotheses#

Hypotheses are testable statements predicting outcomes.

Examples:

  • Models trained with domain adaptation will exhibit reduced fairness degradation under distribution shift.

  • Post-hoc explainability methods will show lower fidelity for deeper architectures.

Not all AI/DS research requires formal hypotheses, but testability and falsifiability remain essential.

4. Novelty and Contribution in AI/DS Research#

Novelty does not require inventing entirely new algorithms. Contributions may include:

  • New empirical evidence

  • Improved experimental design

  • Better evaluation protocols

  • Identification of failure modes

  • Replication and validation under new conditions

  • Application of known methods to underexplored domains

A common pitfall is overestimating novelty based solely on performance improvements.

5. Feasibility and Scope for MS-Level Research#

A Master’s thesis has practical constraints:

  • Limited time

  • Limited computational resources

  • Limited access to proprietary data

A feasible research question should:

  • Be answerable within one academic year

  • Rely on accessible datasets

  • Avoid excessive parameter or dataset explosion

  • Allow incremental progress

Overly ambitious questions often lead to incomplete or superficial research.

6. Aligning Questions with Data and Methods#

Research questions must be compatible with:

  • Available datasets (size, quality, labels)

  • Appropriate methodologies (experimental, empirical, algorithmic)

  • Evaluation metrics

  • Ethical and legal constraints

A mismatch between question and data is a frequent cause of failed studies.

7. Advisor Alignment and Research Planning#

Successful research is collaborative. Early alignment with an advisor helps ensure:

  • Methodological soundness

  • Appropriate scope

  • Feasible expectations

  • Intellectual coherence

A well-formulated research question facilitates productive advisor feedback and committee evaluation.

Key Takeaways#

  • Broad interests must be transformed into specific research problems.

  • Strong research questions are clear, focused, feasible, and grounded in literature.

  • Distinguishing questions, objectives, and hypotheses improves scientific clarity.

  • Novelty in AI/DS often comes from methodology and insight, not only new models.

  • Proper scoping is essential for MS-level research success.

Reflection Questions#

  1. What distinguishes a research problem from an application idea in AI/DS?

  2. How can overly broad research questions undermine scientific rigor?

  3. In what ways can empirical validation constitute a meaningful contribution?

  4. How do feasibility constraints shape good research questions?

  5. How early should advisor alignment occur, and why?

Exercises#

Exercise 1 — Problem Refinement#

Select a broad AI/DS topic and iteratively refine it into:

  • A research problem

  • One primary research question

  • Two supporting objectives

Explain each refinement step.

Exercise 2 — Hypothesis Formulation#

For a chosen research question, propose:

  • One testable hypothesis

  • One alternative hypothesis

  • An outline of how the hypothesis could be falsified

Exercise 3 — Feasibility Assessment#

Evaluate your proposed research question against:

  • Available datasets

  • Required computational resources

  • Time constraints

  • Ethical considerations

Summarize potential risks and mitigation strategies.
